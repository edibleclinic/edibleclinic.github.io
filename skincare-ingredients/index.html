Skincare Ingredients Decoded

What are the most popular skincare ingredients?

This is the start of a series giving you the low-down on the key active ingredients popular in skincare today.

Let’s start with niacinamide.

Also known as 3-Pyridine Carboxamide, niacinamide is a form of Vitamin B3.

There is no definitive clinical evidence for its efficacy as a topical ingredient in skincare. But there is some evidence to suggest an association between niacinamide and skin brightening, controlling hyperpigmentation, sebum control, rosacea and redness, inflammation, acne, fine lines, and anti-ageing in general.

What is not so well known, however, is that you need to have a certain percentage concentration of niacinamide in order to achieve each of these effects. As a general guide:

  • 2% niacinamide will help brighten skin and reduce sebum production, but not much else. We recommend COSRX Galactomyces 95 Tone Balancing Essence.
  • 4% niacinamide is needed to tackle inflammation, acne and rosacea.
  • higher concentrations (5%-10%) are required to fade any dark spots, post inflammatory hyperpigmentation, and freckles. These higher concentrations are also associated with anti-aging, as it may stimulate ceramide synthesis. We recommend The Ordinary Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1%, but only apply it directly onto the offending areas, not all over your face, to minimise the risk of irritation!
  • There’s not much evidence that concentrations higher than 10% do anything other than increasing potential side effects…so definitiely a case of everything in moderation!

Side Effects

Niacinamide can help relieve erythema (redness) and inflammation, but it can conversely be a major CAUSE of flushing and irritation! Some tips to help avoid this:

  • start with lower concentrations (2%) and work your way up slowly.
  • DON’T mix niacinamide with Vitamin C, AHA/BHAs and other direct acids,
  • this one is so crucial: check the ingredients list of your existing skincare products for which ones contain niacinamide (even if it isn’t advertised) to make sure you aren’t inadvertently mixing niacinamide with acids! See our post on What Not To Mix for more info.
